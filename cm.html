Consciousness
Mechanics (CM)

Consciousness = regulation of organism viability.

Consciousness Mechanics (CM) is a biology-first framing of consciousness as a continuous organism-level control architecture. On this view, conscious systems detect change, evaluate its significance for organism viability, select a response, act, and update their condition.

CM is the umbrella framing for the wider model. It introduces the core definition and control logic that later branches into CCM as the comparative capability hierarchy and AoE as the experiential branch.

The core definition

CM begins from a simple claim: consciousness is regulation of organism viability. Consciousness is not something added once a system becomes sufficiently complex. It is the control architecture through which an organism maintains itself in the face of change.

On this view, consciousness is not best approached as a mysterious property, a late cognitive add-on, or a special ingredient layered on top of biology. It is a continuous organism-level control process.

What CM explains

CM approaches conscious systems as control architectures embedded within living organisms. Organisms must continuously regulate interaction with both external environments and internal physiological conditions in order to maintain viability over time. On this view, consciousness is not introduced as an extra property layered on top of biology, but as part of the regulatory machinery through which organisms manage organism–environment coupling.

The aim of CM is to provide a disciplined framing that can scale across biological complexity. It asks what control architecture must exist for environment-coupled behaviour, regulation, persistence, and later reflective capacities to occur, while keeping those claims explicit, conservative, and biologically grounded.

The core loop

Continuous control cycle
  • State — the organism’s current condition
  • Perturbation — detected change in organism or environment
  • Evaluation — whether that change moves toward or away from organism viability
  • Selection — the response or behavioural bias triggered
  • Action — effector output or movement
  • Updated state — the resulting new condition
Primer shorthand

State → Perturbation → Evaluation → Selection → Action → Updated state

This loop is organised around organism viability. What changes across systems is not the existence of a different mechanism, but the depth, coordination, and temporal reach of the same underlying control process.

A concrete example

A hand touches a hot surface:

  • State: tissue intact
  • Perturbation: rapid heat increase
  • Evaluation: movement away from organism viability
  • Selection: withdraw
  • Action: muscles contract
  • Updated state: hand removed, damage limited

Nothing additional is required. There is no separate stage where “pain is experienced” and then another stage where “a decision is made to act”.

The state change is the signal. The evaluation biases response. The action resolves the perturbation. What are often treated as “experience” and “action” are different aspects of the same organism-level control process.

No new mechanism appears

The difference between simple and complex systems is not the appearance of a new kind of process. It is the extension of the same loop.

Capability scaling
  • Reflex — immediate control
  • Regulation — ongoing state management
  • Awareness — selection and gating of signals for broader evaluation
  • Cognition — model-based control over possible future states
  • Planning — extended temporal organisation of behaviour
What increases
  • How much of organism and environment can be represented
  • How many processes can be coordinated
  • How far ahead control can extend

At every level, the underlying structure remains the same: State → Change → Evaluation → Response.

Key distinctions

What it is
  • A biology-first framing of consciousness as regulation of organism viability
  • A way of describing control architecture across life without assuming human-like cognition
  • A framework grounded in explicit mechanism, state, timing, gating, and causal flow
What it is not
  • Not a theory of subjective experience in itself
  • Not a claim that all organisms possess awareness, cognition, or report-like access
  • Not a neuron-bound or taxonomy-bound theory of consciousness

Where CM breaks from common assumptions

Many approaches assume that experience is something generated, cognition is something separate, and action is something that follows. That framing creates unnecessary problems: how experiences combine, how experience causes action, or where consciousness “enters” the system.

CM removes those problems by removing the assumption. There are no separate components to bind. There is only continuous organism-level control organised around the regulation of organism viability.

How CM relates to the wider framework

CM is the umbrella framing. It defines the broad architectural target: consciousness understood as organism-level control organised around the regulation of organism viability.

Within that broader framing, CCM provides the formal comparative model. CCM specifies layered capability boundaries, classification logic, and explicit control commitments using cumulative components, together with the H and K scales for temporal horizon and structural capacity.

AoE sits alongside this as the experiential branch of the framework. AoE does not explain the whole control architecture. It explains how experience is structured within it, including the roles of global state, feeling, valence, awareness, cognition, and emotion.

What experience is within this framework

AoE alignment
  • Feeling corresponds to change in state across a transition
  • Valence corresponds to the direction of change relative to organism viability
  • Awareness corresponds to which changes are selected for broader evaluation
  • Cognition corresponds to model-based evaluation of those changes
Key point

Experience is not constructed from smaller subjective units.

It is the system-level manifestation of organism-level change being evaluated and acted upon within the broader control loop.

Scope boundaries

CM is deliberately broader than awareness and broader than cognition. Under this framing, awareness is treated as a later capability regime in which integrated organism–environment signals become available for comparative monitoring, while cognition refers to later reflective or model-based processing operating within an aware regime.

This distinction is central to the framework. CM does not define consciousness as reportability, global broadcast, or reflective thought. It defines consciousness at the level of organism-level viability regulation, allowing later capacities to be introduced only when the evidence requires them.

CM also maintains a control–execution boundary. It specifies control commitments such as gating, suppression, saturation, prioritisation, and bias, while leaving detailed effector physics and biomechanical implementation to the substrate level.

How the architecture scales

As systems become more capable, three dimensions expand:

  • Representational depth — what can be tracked
  • Coordination complexity — what can be integrated
  • Temporal horizon — how far ahead evaluation can extend

Within CCM, these dimensions are formalised through cumulative capability structure, including the H scale for temporal horizon and the K scale for structural capacity.

These are not separate systems. They are measures of how far the same control architecture has been extended.

Design principles

Architectural discipline
  • Layered capability: higher capabilities build on lower ones and do not replace them
  • Conservative introduction: new commitments appear only when unavoidable
  • Explicit state and timing: control claims must be mechanistically legible
Comparative scope
  • All-life applicability: classification is not restricted to familiar animal models
  • Taxonomy is metadata: organisms are placed by demonstrated control architecture, not by lineage prestige
  • Operational comparability: temporal and structural commitments are made explicit through H and K

A change in the question

The relevant question is not:

“How does consciousness arise?”

The more useful question is:

“What level of this control architecture is present?”

Closing position

From reflex withdrawal to abstract reasoning, the underlying mechanism does not change. What changes is its depth, coordination, and reach through time.

Consciousness is not built from pieces. It is not added to matter. It is the continuous regulation of organism viability through organism-level control.

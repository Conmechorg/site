H Scale - Temporal Horizon

H expresses how far ahead behaviour can be regulated in behaviour-class tick units. It is the temporal horizon axis used within CCM / SCCA to classify future-relevant control depth.

The H scale is not a measure of intelligence, complexity, or generic planning ability. It specifies the temporal reach of control: whether behaviour is governed only by the present, by near-future extrapolation, by persistent state carried across episodes, by future needs within the organism’s lifespan, or by representations of times beyond lived experience.

H levels must be read as bounded architectural commitments. They describe how far behaviour can be organised across time, not how cognitively sophisticated an organism appears in human terms.

What H represents

The H scale represents temporal control reach. It expresses how far ahead an organism’s control architecture can regulate behaviour for the behaviour class under analysis.

This is not primarily about physical duration, wall-clock time, or whether an organism can verbally describe the future. It is about whether control is limited to the present tick — the organism’s minimum effective control update interval — or whether it extends into anticipated immediate outcomes, extrapolated future states, maintained anticipatory state across ticks, post-event carried-forward state shaped by delayed effects, future needs within the organism’s lifespan, or even representations of times outside lived experience.

H therefore provides a bounded comparative representation of future-relevant control depth, expressed relative to the control cadence of the behavioural regime being analysed.

How to read H

Temporal axis, not cognition ladder

H is a temporal axis within the wider framework. A higher H level does not automatically imply greater intelligence, greater complexity, richer awareness, or human-like planning. It only means that behaviour depends on a longer temporal horizon of control.

Bounded by architecture

H levels are governed by K — the system’s structural capacity — and by component definitions. An H assignment must be justified by the internal control commitments required for the behaviour, not by loose interpretation of surface performance.

The H levels

H0
Immediate reactive (No horizon)
Control is governed entirely by current sensory conditions (within-tick integration only).
H1
Short trajectory extrapolation (Short Horizon)
Behaviour is modulated by a bounded single-trajectory imminence estimate for trigger timing within a limited number of ticks (no branching futures, no valuation).
H2
Anticipatory State (Extended Horizon)
Behaviour may be regulated across multiple future tick cycles via anticipatory mobilisation and maintenance of persistent control state (e.g., global readiness) that shapes subsequent execution conditions, without requiring branching futures, valuation, or planning.
H3
Post Event Persistent State (Extended effect-window horizon)
Previously acquired information persists across episodes, shaping future responses even when the triggering stimulus is absent and reflecting the delayed effects of prior outcomes.
H4
Long Horizon Planning (Extended organism-scale horizon)
Behaviour may be influenced by anticipated outcomes spanning significant portions of the organism’s lifespan.
H5+
Higher-order Abstraction (Beyond lifespan horizons)
Behaviour influenced by futures beyond the organism’s lifespan (e.g. lineage, culture, abstract futures).

Why the distinctions matter

The H scale is useful because it prevents very different kinds of temporal control from being collapsed into a single vague idea of “planning” or “intelligence”. Reacting to heat, extrapolating the future position of a moving target, maintaining anticipatory readiness across ticks, carrying forward a post-event persistent state, preparing for winter, and reasoning about times beyond one’s own life are not the same kind of temporal achievement.

Treating these as distinct horizons makes behavioural comparison more precise, and helps separate temporal reach from other axes such as structural capacity or component depth.

H and tick

H is expressed in tick units. Here, a tick is the organism’s minimum effective control update interval for the behaviour class under analysis: the shortest cadence at which control-relevant state can be sensed, integrated, and used to commit or dispatch one or more response commands for that behavioural class.

Tick boundaries are therefore defined by control-cycle completion — commit or dispatch — rather than by fixed external time slices, and not by requiring that a physical motor programme has finished.

A single organism may exhibit multiple effective tick scales across different behavioural classes, such as fast defensive reflexes versus slower developmental, provisioning, or seasonal regulation.

In other words, H is always interpreted relative to the control cadence of the specific behavioural regime being analysed.

What H is and is not

What it is
  • A temporal classification axis for future-relevant control depth
  • A way to express how far ahead behaviour can be regulated in behaviour-class ticks
  • A bounded classificatory axis used alongside CCM components and K scale constraints
What it is not
  • Not a generic planning score
  • Not a synonym for intelligence, complexity, or imagination
  • Not a free-standing behavioural label detached from mechanism

K Scale - Structural Capacity

K is a conceptual scale indicating the level of internal processing capability that must be present for an organism to exhibit a given class of behaviour. Within CCM / SCCA, it functions as the structural capacity axis.

K is not a direct measure of intelligence, neuron count, or generic complexity. It is a way of asking what must persist internally for behaviour to move from rigid same-input same-response patterns, through sensitivity shifts, differentiated response by input type, internal-state control, learning from outcomes, regime switching, and finally explicit internal modelling.

K levels should be read as bounded capability commitments. They do not rank organisms by worth or smartness. They indicate the level of internal structural and processing organisation required for the behaviour being considered.

What K represents

The K scale represents structural capacity for behaviour. It is a conceptual way of indicating the level of internal processing and persistence that must be available for a particular class of behaviour to occur.

The emphasis is not on how impressive an organism looks, nor on raw brain size, hardware mass, or neuron count. The question is simpler and more precise: what must exist internally for this behaviour to be possible at all?

That is why K is framed through behavioural examples. It helps distinguish organisms or systems that always respond the same way, from those that can modulate intensity, treat different inputs differently, switch behaviour according to internal state or context, learn from outcomes, or explicitly evaluate alternative futures before acting.

How to read K

Capability axis, not intelligence ladder

K is not a ladder of worth, intelligence, or consciousness in the everyday sense. A higher K level only means that a more demanding class of internal processing capability must be present for the behaviour under discussion.

Behaviour-first interpretation

K is best understood by asking what new behavioural flexibility appears at each step: identical response, then altered response strength, then differentiated response by input type, then internal state control, then learning from outcomes, then regime-level changes, switching, and explicit evaluation of future outcomes.

The K levels

K0
No persistent structure
The same input produces the same response each time, with no retained variation or internal carry-forward affecting what happens next.
K1
Minimal persistent scalars
Response strength reflects recent history, so the same kind of disturbance can produce a weaker or stronger reaction on repetition.
K2
Indexed persistent state
Different kinds of inputs can carry different stored effects, allowing the organism to respond differently depending on what it encounters.
K3
Global control state
Internal state can override identical surroundings, so the same environment yields different behaviour depending on conditions such as hunger.
K4
Calibration from outcomes
Behaviour updates from success and failure, allowing future attempts to be altered by what happened on previous attempts.
K5
Regime-level control
Behaviour changes by mode or regime, such as shifting from food-seeking to hiding when threat context becomes dominant.
K6
Multiple regimes and switching
Competing drives can alternate over time, so behaviour switches between regimes as context changes and then changes back again.
K7
Explicit internal modelling
Behaviour is guided by imagined future outcomes, allowing evaluation of risky versus safe routes before a final action is chosen.

Why the distinctions matter

The K scale matters because very different kinds of behavioural flexibility are often lumped together under vague words such as “intelligence”, “complexity”, or “learning”. But identical response, recent-history modulation, differentiated treatment of inputs, internal-state control, outcome-based updating, regime change, switching, and explicit internal modelling are not the same thing.

By separating these levels, K helps make comparison more disciplined. It clarifies what class of internal capability is actually being implied when we attribute a behaviour to an organism or system.

K and persistent internal capability

K is about what must persist internally across time for behaviour to be shaped by more than the immediate present. At lower levels this may amount to little or no retained variation. At higher levels it includes state that can bias behaviour, differentiate between input classes, encode prior outcomes, support regime switching, or contribute to explicit internal modelling.

The important point is functional rather than anatomical: not merely that some substrate exists, but that it supports behaviourally relevant carry-forward from one moment to the next.

K and world-model structure

The K scale does not assume that organisms possess rich world models at low levels. Most of the scale is about simpler forms of persistence and behavioural organisation: repeated-response effects, input-specific differences, internal-state bias, learning from outcomes, and mode switching.

Only at the highest end of the illustrated public interpretation does K move into behaviour that is best explained by explicit internal modelling of possible future outcomes.

What K is and is not

What it is
  • A conceptual structural-capacity axis for behaviour
  • An indication of the internal processing capability required for a behavioural class
  • A way of distinguishing different kinds of persistent internal organisation
What it is not
  • Not an intelligence score
  • Not a neuron-count proxy or brain-size ranking
  • Not a claim that rich world models exist at all levels
